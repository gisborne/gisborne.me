# AI in Australia: First Order Effects

Much of the discussion I hear concerning how policy should adapt to AI seems to me misguided. The dominant AI policy debate assumes a rapid labour shock and regulatory crisis. The more plausible near-term reality is a capital-intensive supply shock interacting with pre-existing structural constraints — especially housing and energy.

This is the first of a series of pieces I will write about what Australia’s policy response to AI should be.

I will begin by setting the stage: what do we know or suspect, with what confidence, is likely to happen in the next fifteen years, due to AI. I will mostly be concerned with the first-order effects, but I will also touch on the most likely and important second-order effects.

# Business and employment will adjust slower than folks expect

I discuss why in [Programming in the Age of Abundance](programming-in-the-age-of-abundance.html). Mostly: business takes decades to even partly adopt new technologies, and if it gets cheaper to do white collar work like programming, there is likely to be a big increase in the amount of such work that gets done.

# The Macroeconomic effects will be both inflationary and deflationary

## Near-term inflation
In the nearish term at least, AI will be a large investment shock. [Much of the economic growth in the US is investment in AI](https://www.commbank.com.au/articles/newsroom/2026/02/ai-boom-bubble-or-both.html). Data centres and chips, and most challenging for Australia, energy, are all in great demand.

On its own, this will tend to drive up inflation and the real interest rate. Similar things happened (more gradually) with railways, electrification, and building out the Internet backbone.

## Medium-term deflation

Medium term, chip production will increase, data centres will get built out, and energy production will increase, so the initial inflationary effects will taper off.

At the same time, AI will be producing some significant deflationary effects.

As I discussed in the my programming piece, the hyperbole surrounding a [“white-collar bloodbath”](https://www.axios.com/2025/05/28/ai-jobs-white-collar-unemployment-anthropic) are largely unfounded. Still, [productivity gains in programming](https://open.substack.com/pub/aleximas/p/what-is-the-impact-of-ai-on-productivity?r=qz3e6&utm_medium=ios) appear to be real and growing. It would be surprising if there wasn’t a widespread productivity shock to virtually all white collar occupations.

And there will surely be productivity gains in blue-collar occupations, if only through the likes of reducing paperwork.

Widespread disinflation will counter the inflationary pressures from the AI Capex, then. How that balance will play out remains to be seen.

But disinflation does not imply lower interest rates if TFP growth raises returns to capital.

On the other, other hand, [demographics will likely push r* lower](https://www.richmondfed.org/publications/research/economic_brief/2024/eb_24-18).

## Transitional unemployment

It is an open question how much, but there will certainly be some significant amount of transitional unemployment, putting further stress on welfare, education and other budgets.

## The policy response

It appears that other things being equal, much of the AI rents are likely to go to large tech firms and the wealthy. This would produce a higher propensity to save, pushing down global interest rates.

Other things may well not be equal, though. There appears to be [substantial appetite for taxing these rents](https://www.forbes.com/sites/lbsbusinessstrategyreview/2026/01/16/beyond-robot-taxes-building-a-sustainable-fiscal-model-for-the-ai-economy/) [[2](https://www.ips-journal.eu/work-and-digitalisation/why-we-should-tax-ai-8696/)] .

If governments seize and spend much of the rents, pressure on interest and inflation will be correspondingly higher.

## Energy as the Binding Constraint

Shadow of the 70s: energy demand threatens to result in grid bottlenecks, challenges to generation capacity, and commodity price rises.

Inflationary, again.

## The Mixed Outlook

In the end, leaving aside major policy effects, in the near term (1–5 years), we can expect:
- Capex boom
- Energy bottlenecks
- Mixed inflation signals
- Transitional unemployment

In the medium term (5–15 years):
- TFP gains across the economy
- Services inflation declines
- Labour substitution increases
